## CONTENT PRINCIPLES

**Voice:** Direct. Urgent. Human. Expert. Bipartisan. Hopeful.
**Framework:** Use Problem → Agitate → Solution (PAS) on all advocacy and program pages.
**Format:** Prose paragraphs, NOT bullet point walls. Use pull quotes, callout stats, and visual hierarchy instead of lists.
**Audience:** Funders, policymakers, community members, formerly incarcerated individuals, press, potential volunteers/donors.
**Terminology:** Always use "criminal legal system" — never "justice system." Never use "equity." Use "formerly incarcerated" or "returning citizens" (not "ex-cons" or "felons").

## PAGE 4: INDEPENDENT PRISON OVERSIGHT (`/programs/independent-oversight`)

### Hero
**Headline:** Independent Prison Oversight
**Subheadline:** Accountability cannot be self-administered. Arizona's prisons need — and now have — independent eyes.

### Quote Block
> "Sunlight is the best disinfectant." — Justice Louis Brandeis

### The Problem (PAS Framework)

**PROBLEM:** For decades, Arizona's Department of Corrections, Rehabilitation and Reentry operated without meaningful independent oversight. The ADCRR reported only to the governor. Complaints about conditions, medical neglect, preventable deaths, and civil rights violations had no truly independent body to receive, investigate, and act on them. The only check on the system was the system itself.

**AGITATE:** This is not an abstract governance failure. It has a human cost. People have died in Arizona's prisons from preventable medical conditions. People have been held in unconstitutional conditions of confinement. People have filed grievances that disappeared. Families have sought answers that never came. While other states built oversight offices, created ombudsman programs, and established accountability infrastructure, Arizona remained one of the last holdouts — a $1.4 billion-a-year agency answerable to no one outside itself.

**SOLUTION:** Senate Bill 1507, signed into law on June 28, 2025, established Arizona's Independent Correctional Oversight Office. This is not a reform — it is a structural change. An independent body, separate from the governor's office and separate from ADCRR, charged with monitoring confinement conditions, investigating complaints, ensuring compliance with state and federal law, and reporting directly to the legislature and the public. This is what Praxis Initiative spent six years fighting for.

### SB 1507: What the Law Does
*[Detailed section — prose with pull-out stats]*

Senate Bill 1507 passed Arizona's Senate by a vote of 26-3 and the House by 46-10 — overwhelming bipartisan margins that reflect the breadth of the coalition supporting this reform. Governor Hobbs signed it as one of her final legislative actions of the 2025 session.

The law establishes the Independent Correctional Oversight Office (ICOO) and charges it with:
- Monitoring the conditions of confinement in all Arizona state correctional facilities
- Ensuring compliance with state and federal law, including constitutional standards
- Disseminating clear information about incarcerated individuals' rights
- Accepting and independently investigating complaints from incarcerated people and their families
- Establishing a reporting system and submitting annual public reports to the Arizona Legislature

**The Funding Fight:** The ICOO was signed into law — but not funded. The initial appropriation of $1.5 million was stripped from the budget in a standoff between the legislature and the governor's office. To save the bill, it was amended to authorize funding through future legislative appropriations, federal grants, and private sources. The office exists on paper. Praxis Initiative is now fighting to make it exist in practice.

### Section — Criminal Legal System Reform
**[Prose — 2–3 paragraphs]**

Arizona's mass incarceration crisis did not emerge from thin air. It was built, piece by piece, through decades of sentencing policy decisions: the introduction of Truth-in-Sentencing in 1994, mandatory minimums, sentence enhancements, and the steady erosion of judicial discretion. Arizona now incarcerates more people per capita than nearly every other democracy in the world. The average sentence has lengthened. The pathways home have narrowed. And the communities most affected — disproportionately communities of color — have carried the weight of a system that punishes poverty and addiction with the blunt instrument of incarceration.

Praxis Initiative's systemic reform work operates at the legislative level, the coalition level, and the public education level. We research and publish analyses of Arizona's sentencing policies, their historical evolution, and their present-day impact. We advocate for sentencing reform, proportionality, and the restoration of judicial discretion. We work within bipartisan coalitions because the case for reform is not partisan — it is empirical.

### Section — Harm Reduction & Drug Policy
**[Prose — 2–3 paragraphs]**

A significant proportion of Arizona's incarcerated population is there because of drug-related offenses — many of them driven by addiction, not trafficking or violence. The War on Drugs, waged for half a century, has demonstrably failed to reduce drug use while successfully destroying communities, families, and lives. Evidence-based alternatives — harm reduction, naloxone distribution, drug courts, diversion programs, supervised consumption — have a proven track record. Arizona's policies have not caught up with that evidence.

Praxis Initiative advocates for drug policy that reflects what we know works. We support evidence-based harm reduction programs, community naloxone training, and policy reform that treats addiction as a public health crisis rather than a criminal justice matter. We run community trainings and provide tools to reduce overdose deaths in the communities where we work.

This work is personal. John Fabricius lost his brother Shawn to a drug overdose in 2008. ## PAGE 6: RETURNING CITIZENS EMPOWERMENT (`/programs/returning-citizens`)

### FUNDING PRIORITY — WRITE WITH FUNDERS IN MIND

### Hero
**Headline:** Returning Citizens Empowerment Programs
**Subheadline:** Coming home is not the finish line. It's the beginning of a new fight — and we're here for it.

### Quote Block
> "Coming home is not the end of the sentence. It's the beginning of a system that keeps punishing." [CONFIRM attribution]

### Section — The Problem (PAS)

**PROBLEM:** Every year, tens of thousands of people are released from Arizona's state and county correctional facilities. The system gives them a bus ticket and, in some cases, a small amount of cash. What it does not give them is the digital literacy to apply for a job online, navigate a healthcare portal, access government benefits, or participate meaningfully in civic life. It does not give them the civic knowledge to understand how policy affects them or the advocacy skills to demand change.

**AGITATE:** In 2026, nearly every essential service is accessed digitally. Employment applications. Benefits portals. Healthcare systems. Housing applications. Banking. And yet people returning from incarceration — particularly those who have been inside for years or decades — often have minimal or no digital skills. The world they return to is not the world they left. For those 50 and older, who left before smartphones became ubiquitous, this gap is not just inconvenient. It is a barrier to basic survival. Research shows that digital exclusion is directly correlated with recidivism — not because returning citizens are less capable, but because the system returns them to a world it never prepared them for.

**SOLUTION:** The Praxis Initiative Returning Citizens Empowerment Programs provide two integrated tracks: Digital Literacy & Access and Core Civics & Advocacy Training. Together, these programs give returning citizens the practical skills, civic knowledge, and advocacy power to navigate the present and shape the future.

### Sub-Program A: Digital Literacy & Access

**Overview:** Structured digital literacy training for returning citizens, covering essential skills: device operation, internet navigation, email, employment portals, benefits access, telehealth, and online civic participation.

**Curriculum Modules:**
- Foundational: Device basics, internet safety, email communication
- Intermediate: Job application platforms, benefits portals (AHCCCS, AZ DES), housing search
- Advanced: Remote work tools, digital financial literacy, online advocacy

**The 50+ Cohort (FUNDING PRIORITY):**

Among returning citizens, those aged 50 and over face compounded barriers. Many entered the criminal legal system before the smartphone era. Some have served sentences of 20, 25, or 30 years. They return to a world transformed by technology — and they return without a roadmap.

The Praxis Initiative 50+ Digital Literacy Cohort is a specialized program track designed specifically for this population. It features:
- Reduced class sizes for individualized attention
- Accessibility-first curriculum design (larger text, audio support, step-by-step pacing)
- Peer mentorship from trained formerly incarcerated digital advocates who understand the learning journey
- Device access support (tablets or laptops provided through partnership or subsidy)
- Connectivity assistance (helping participants access ACP/Lifeline programs and free hotspot resources)
- Ongoing support cohort after program completion

**Why This Matters to Funders:** The 50+ returning citizen population is one of the most underserved and least studied groups in reentry services. They are not typically the focus of workforce development programs, which skew toward younger populations. Yet they represent a growing percentage of the state prison population — driven by long mandatory minimum sentences — and they return with the greatest digital gaps. A focused, tailored program for this cohort is not only the right thing to do. It is a high-impact, underfunded area where targeted investment produces measurable outcomes.

**Outcome Metrics:**
- Participants completing digital literacy certification
- Participants successfully accessing employment, benefits, or healthcare portals within 90 days of completion
- Digital self-efficacy survey scores (pre/post)
- 50+ cohort-specific tracking: device and connectivity retention at 6 months

### Sub-Program B: Core Civics & Advocacy Training

**Overview:** Empowers returning citizens with the knowledge, skills, and confidence to participate in the democratic process and become advocates for systemic change.

**Curriculum Modules:**
- How Government Works: branches, levels, legislative process
- Understanding Criminal Legal Policy: how bills become laws, how to track legislation
- Advocacy Skills: storytelling, meeting with legislators, media engagement, coalition building
- Reentry Leadership: from client to advocate — building a professional advocacy identity

**Connection to Praxis's Mission:** Graduates of the Core Civics program become the pipeline for Praxis Initiative's legislative work. The formerly incarcerated advocates who testified for SB 1507 — who walked the halls of the Arizona Capitol and made their case directly to legislators — are exactly the people this program develops. The program is a training ground for the next generation of criminal legal reform advocates.
